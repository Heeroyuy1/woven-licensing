Performance Metrics Explained
MetricWhat It MeansHow to Read It
Total Return How much the simulated account grew from start to end, expressed as a percentage of the original capital. Higher = better. A +500% return means $100K became $600K. Negative means the strategy lost money.
Sharpe Ratio Return relative to risk. Measures how much return you got per unit of volatility (price swings). Above 1.0 = decent. Above 2.0 = excellent. Below 0 = worse than holding cash. Higher Sharpe means smoother, more consistent returns.
Max Drawdown (Max DD) The largest peak-to-trough drop the account experienced. A 20% max DD means at some point the account was down 20% from its all-time high. Lower = safer. Under 10% is conservative. 20-30% is moderate. Above 50% means the strategy had severe losing periods.
Win Rate Percentage of trades that were profitable (closed above entry for longs, below for shorts). 50%+ is typical for trend strategies. A high win rate (80%+) with low returns may mean winners are small and losers are cut quickly. A low win rate (30%) can still be profitable if winners are much larger than losers.
Total Trades How many complete round-trip trades (entry + exit) were executed during the backtest. More trades = more statistical confidence in the results. Under 30 trades may be too few to draw conclusions.
Realized P&L The actual dollar profit/loss from all closed trades combined. This is your bottom line. Positive = strategy made money. Tracks the change from initial capital to final equity.
P/L Ratio Average winning trade size divided by average losing trade size. Above 1.0 means winners are bigger than losers on average. Even with a 40% win rate, a 3:1 P/L ratio can be highly profitable.
Pattern Types Explained
Pattern TypeWhat Harper DetectsPlain-Language Translation
seasonal Consistent price behavior during specific months across multiple years. Uses binomial tests to determine if positive/negative months occur more often than random chance. "This stock tends to go up (or down) during [month] — it happened in X out of Y years. This is statistically significant (p < 0.05), meaning it's unlikely to be random noise."
day_of_week Specific weekdays where returns are consistently different from zero. Uses t-tests to see if average daily returns on that day are statistically meaningful. "On [day], this stock's average daily change is meaningfully different from flat — suggesting a recurring weekly rhythm in how it trades."
volume_spike Days where trading volume was more than 2x the 20-day average, and what happened to the price over the next 1-5 days after those spikes. "When this stock has unusually heavy trading volume, the price tends to [go up/down] the next day. Heavy volume often signals institutional interest or news-driven moves."
technical_setup RSI extremes (oversold below 30, overbought above 70) and the 5-day forward return after those signals trigger. Uses binomial tests to see if the forward direction is non-random. "When RSI drops below 30 (oversold), the stock historically rebounds over the next week X% of the time. When RSI rises above 70 (overbought), the stock historically pulls back or continues trending depending on direction found."
How to Read Patterns (Ledger)
ColumnWhat It MeansHow to Interpret
Confidence A score from 0-100% representing how statistically reliable the pattern is. Based on p-value from significance testing. >80% = High confidence (very unlikely to be random). 60-80% = Moderate. <60% = Low confidence — use cautiously.
Type The category of pattern Harper discovered. See Pattern Types Guide above for detailed explanations. Each type uses different detection methods. Seasonal = calendar-based. Technical = indicator-based. Volume = activity-based.
Symbol The stock ticker where this pattern was detected. Patterns are symbol-specific. A seasonal pattern in AAPL does not apply to TSLA.
Direction Whether the pattern historically led to bullish (price-up) or bearish (price-down) outcomes. Bullish patterns suggest buying opportunities. Bearish patterns suggest caution or short-selling setups.
Win Rate How often the pattern's predicted direction was correct historically. 60%+ = consistently correct. 50-60% = slight edge. Under 50% = the opposite direction happened more often.
Probability The historical frequency of the pattern occurring. A 70% probability means the pattern appeared in 7 out of 10 possible opportunities. Higher probability = more frequent event. A rare event (low probability) still matters if it's highly profitable when it does occur.
P-Value Statistical measure of whether the pattern could be random noise. Lower = more significant. p < 0.05 = statistically significant (95%+ confidence it's not random). p < 0.01 = highly significant. p > 0.05 = may be random — treat with caution.
Description Human-readable summary of what the pattern is, including historical counts and average outcomes. Read this first to understand the pattern at a glance. Example: "META volume spikes: next day avg -0.97% (bearish), 9/15 follow direction" means 15 spike days occurred, 9 went down, average drop was 0.97%.
Important Context

Correlation is not causation. Every pattern Harper finds is a statistical relationship observed in historical data. These patterns may or may not repeat in the future.

Backtest results are simulated. The performance numbers shown are from paper trading with virtual money. They assume perfect fills at market prices with small slippage. Real trading involves spreads, commissions, and liquidity constraints that can reduce returns.

Sample size matters. Patterns with fewer than 10 occurrences should be treated as exploratory, not actionable. The more data points, the more reliable the statistical inference.

Market regime changes. Relationships that held from 2020-2025 may break down if market structure changes (e.g., interest rate shifts, regulatory changes, new market participants). Harper provides historical context — not guarantees.
